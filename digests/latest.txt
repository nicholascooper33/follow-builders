No Priors: AI for Atoms — How Periodic Labs is Revolutionizing Materials Engineering with Liam Fedus

Takeaway: Liam Fedus — co-creator of ChatGPT and former VP of Post-Training at OpenAI — left the frontier language model world to build Periodic Labs, betting that the next great scientific unlock is connecting AI to the physical world of atoms, materials, and chemistry.

Fedus's background traces a distinctive arc: physics undergrad researching dark matter, then Google Brain starting in 2016 during the formative era of transformers and mixture-of-experts architectures, then OpenAI where he helped productionize GPT-4 into what became ChatGPT. He describes the ChatGPT launch as a "starting gun," but is blunt that the technology of late 2022 was still too immature to build Periodic on. What changed: reasoning models, test-time inference, reliable tool use, and the rise of coding agents. Those capabilities — not raw language understanding — made it finally viable to connect AI to real laboratory experimentation.

The core thesis is that science cannot happen purely in the digital realm. As Fedus put it: "Science ultimately isn't sitting in a room thinking really hard. You have to conduct experiments. You have to learn from them. You have to interface with reality." Periodic Labs builds a closed-loop system that cycles between AI inference, physics simulation, and wet-lab experiments — not consuming a static dataset, but actively driving the next experiment based on what the previous one revealed.

On data quality, Fedus is candid: one of his engineers found that a reported material property from the literature spanned "many orders of magnitude" across published sources, making that data nearly useless as training signal without experimental grounding. Periodic starts with the world knowledge baked into open-source models — including strong coding capabilities from tools like Claude Code, which Fedus credits as "a huge accelerator for the company" — then fine-tunes on curated experimental data in specific chemical spaces, achieving high sample efficiency because the model already has a strong prior on the world.

Fedus's ten-year vision: giving humanity "agency for atomic rearrangement and synthesis" — materials discovery moving at even a fraction of the pace the software world moves — with profound implications for semiconductors, aerospace, and energy infrastructure.
