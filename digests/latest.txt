Aaron Levie — CEO at Box
After a week on the road meeting dozens of enterprise IT and AI leaders across banking, media, healthcare, and retail, Aaron Levie shared his most detailed field report yet on where enterprise AI actually stands. The headline shift: companies are moving from "chat era" AI to agents that use tools, process data, and execute real work — and the "let a thousand flowers bloom" experimentation phase is giving way to targeted automation in specific workflows. The practical blockers remain change management (one company deployed a head of AI in every business unit just to stay coordinated) and legacy infrastructure that agents can't easily plug into. On jobs: almost no one is talking about replacing headcount — the dominant use cases are things companies simply couldn't do before. And crucially, Levie argues engineers are not going away; the technical complexity of actually standing up agent systems (skills, MCP, CLIs) means technical people will be more indispensable than ever, not less.
https://x.com/levie/status/2043426157367095397

In a separate tweet, Levie invoked the Jevons paradox for security: better AI tooling for finding vulnerabilities will increase demand for security talent, not decrease it. AI surfaces findings 100x faster, but expert judgment is still required to triage, remediate, and make architectural decisions. "Autonomous exploitability automates the proving step, but it doesn't automate the response."

Garry Tan — President & CEO at Y Combinator
Garry Tan distilled the most useful mental model for agentic engineering he's learned this year: push smart, fuzzy, human-style operations into markdown skills ("fat skills"); push must-be-perfect deterministic operations into code ("fat code"); and keep the harness itself thin. It's a clean architectural principle that explains why well-structured AI agents are both powerful and maintainable. Separately, he offered a crisp maxim for avoiding over-engineering: "Do the right job at the right layer. Everything else is architecture astronomy."

Latent Space — Marc Andreessen on The Death of the Browser, Pi + OpenClaw, and Why "This Time Is Different"

Takeaway: Marc Andreessen argues that Pi + OpenClaw represent one of the ten most important software breakthroughs in history — essentially the marriage of the Unix shell mindset with large language models — and that this architecture makes AI agents genuinely, profoundly different from anything that came before.

Andreessen opens by pushing back on AI hype cycles, calling the current moment an "80-year overnight success": decades of deep research in neural networks, transformers, and compute suddenly unlocking, not a bolt from the blue. The real knees in the curve were AlexNet (2013) and the Transformer (2017); everything since has been a cascade from those foundations.

The most technically dense portion of the episode covers what Andreessen calls the conceptual breakthrough behind Pi and OpenClaw. Drawing on his Unix roots — he was coding in Lisp in 1989 during the last AI boom — he explains that the old Unix mindset was about composing discrete modules through a shell, rather than building monolithic castles in the sky like IBM's OS/360. What Pi and OpenClaw figured out, he argues, is that an agent is simply: LLM + Unix shell + filesystem + markdown + cron. Every component other than the model was already fully understood. And crucially, because agent state lives in files rather than in the model itself, you can swap the underlying LLM and your agent retains all its memories and capabilities. "It was like swapping out a chip and recompiling."

On the death of the browser: Andreessen reflects on the design choices he made building Mosaic, including the deliberate decision to use verbose text protocols (HTTP, HTML) over compressed binary ones, betting on infinite future bandwidth. He calls it the original "tokenmaxxing." The parallel he draws: just as view-source let anyone learn to build web pages, the transparency of agent architectures creates a similar latent power for builders today. The capability unlock of giving an agent access to a full browser on top of a shell is "profound" — and he predicts everyone will eventually have a family of agents.

One direct quote that captures his enthusiasm: "If I were 18, this is 100% what I would be spending all of my time on. This is such an incredible conceptual breakthrough."

Claude Blog — Preparing your security program for AI-accelerated offense
Published: April 10, 2026

Anthropic's Project Glasswing — which deploys Claude Mythos Preview for defensive cybersecurity — generated a companion post aimed at practical security teams. The core thesis: within the next 24 months, AI will find and chain into working exploits the vast majority of currently unpatched bugs, collapsing the window between patch release and active exploitation. The good news is this works both ways.

The recommendations are prioritized by what has been shown to hold versus degrade under AI-accelerated offense:
- Close your patch gap immediately, starting with the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog; use EPSS scores to prioritize the rest. Internet-facing systems should be patched within 24 hours of an exploit becoming available.
- Prepare for an order-of-magnitude increase in vulnerability reports. Manual triage processes and weekly spreadsheet reviews will not scale — automation with humans in the loop is becoming necessary.
- Audit open-source dependencies using OpenSSF Scorecard and reduce redundant libraries (multiple HTTP clients, multiple JSON parsers) that give attackers more surface area for no functional gain.
- Use frontier models to assist with triage, deduplication, remediation ticket drafting, and even generating patches for well-described vulnerabilities.

The post maps directly onto SOC 2 and ISO 27001 controls and is framed as living guidance that will be updated as Project Glasswing's work continues.
