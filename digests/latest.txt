No Priors — "SAP: Bringing the 'Operating System' of a Company into the AI Era with CTO Philipp Herzig"
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5u7AjPardvo

Takeaway: AI adoption in the enterprise is falling further behind the AI innovation pace — and closing that gap requires treating AI as a business model transformation, not just a technology upgrade.

SAP CTO Philipp Herzig sat down with No Priors to explain why SAP — with 400,000 enterprise customers and software spanning finance, HR, supply chain, and logistics — thinks about AI differently from most. Herzig's core argument: the gap between the "AI innovation race" and the "AI outcome race" is widening, not shrinking. Delivering outcomes requires not just better models, but better data infrastructure, reliable evals, and a willingness to reengineer the entire software stack.

He laid out three layers undergoing transformation: the UI (moving from static interfaces designed for human clicks to generative UIs that respond to natural language queries against live business data), business processes (agents blending structured and unstructured data to handle tasks like travel expense automation and consulting support), and the data layer (SAP is building a harmonized semantic knowledge graph to give AI reliable grounding in real enterprise data).

On LLMs versus predictive models, Herzig made a point that cuts against the dominant narrative: large language models are not well suited for tabular, predictive tasks like demand forecasting or cash flow prediction. SAP's research led to RPT-1 (Relational Pretrained Transformers), a new architecture built specifically for structured/tabular prediction that could democratize what used to require specialized data scientists.

Perhaps the most resonant moment: "Do you still remember test-driven development? Nobody did it because it was more fun to write the code first. Now the behavior and the writing of the code is so much automated — you need to describe the right outcome." Writing evals is the new test-driven development, and enterprises that learn to do it well will compound their AI advantage.

On pricing: SAP is shifting from seat-based to a consumptive hybrid model, but customers want predictability — so the transition is gradual. Herzig was candid that nobody knows how fast outcome-based licensing will arrive.

Direct quote: "AI is only as powerful as the data is. The biggest challenge is not the AI — it's teaching the AI to do the right thing at scale."

Anthropic Engineering — "Scaling Managed Agents: Decoupling the brain from the hands"
https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/managed-agents

Anthropic's engineering team published an architectural deep dive on how they built Managed Agents — their hosted service for long-horizon agents. The core insight is a classic distributed systems move: decouple the "brain" (Claude + its harness) from the "hands" (sandboxes and tools) and the "session" (the append-only event log). This separates concerns that were previously tangled, and lets each component fail or be replaced independently.

The gains were substantial. Removing the container assumption cut time-to-first-token latency by 60% at p50 and over 90% at p95, because containers no longer need to be provisioned upfront for every session. The security model also improved: credentials never touch the sandbox where Claude's generated code runs, eliminating a class of prompt-injection attacks where a compromised session could steal tokens and spawn unrestricted new sessions.

The session log also doubles as a durable context store that lives outside Claude's context window — allowing long-horizon tasks to checkpoint, rewind, and resume without irreversible compaction decisions destroying information that future turns might need.

The piece is worth reading alongside Anthropic's prior work on context engineering and harness design, as it shows how assumptions baked into harnesses (like "context anxiety" workarounds that became dead weight when Claude Opus 4.5 no longer needed them) must be continually questioned as models improve.
