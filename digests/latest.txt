Andrej Karpathy — former Director of AI at Tesla, founding team at OpenAI

Karpathy shared a detailed breakdown of how he's using LLMs to build personal knowledge bases — indexing source documents into a raw/ directory, then having the LLM compile a wiki of .md files with summaries, backlinks, and concept articles. He uses Obsidian as his IDE frontend and runs complex Q&A sessions against wikis that have grown to ~100 articles and ~400K words, finding he doesn't need fancy RAG since the LLM auto-maintains index files and reads related data well at that scale. He noted a large fraction of his recent token throughput has shifted from code manipulation to knowledge manipulation — outputs rendered as markdown files, slideshows (Marp format), or matplotlib images. In a follow-up, he extrapolated the natural endpoint: every query to a frontier LLM spawns a team of LLMs that iteratively construct an ephemeral wiki, lint it, loop several times, and produce a full report — far beyond a simple .decode().

Aaron Levie — CEO at Box

Levie shared one of the sharpest architectural lessons from building AI agents: you have to be brutally unsentimental. As models improve, the scaffolding you built to compensate for their earlier limitations becomes redundant — or even harmful — and you must ruthlessly discard it to unlock the new performance gains. He described the recurring loop: build scaffolding around the LLM → model capabilities dramatically improve, rendering systems redundant → remove prior scaffolding to capture new gains → new capabilities unlock a harder set of problems → repeat from step 1. In building Box Agent, his team had to evolve multiple components of their agent harness as models got dramatically better at complex reasoning, tool use, and large context window handling. Many mitigations they'd built for finding data or chunking text actually produced lower quality results once models improved. The main lesson: always chase frontier capabilities and don't become nostalgic about tech you already built.

Ryo Lu — Designer at Cursor

Ryo announced the launch of Cursor 3, framing the new design philosophy as "glass vs. black box." The argument: the terminal was a black box, AI kept that pattern and made it more addictive — you type a wish, pull the lever, accept or reject the whole thing. Cursor 3 breaks the box. The PM writes a plan and watches it become real; the designer sketches an idea and iterates live in code; the new programmer reads every diff and builds intuition. Agents are visible, diffs are shown, plans are editable, state is clear — nothing hides, nothing is forced. As AI gets more powerful, glass gets more important — not because you need to watch every move, but because the best work happens when you know you can intervene, and "the best outcomes come from you and the model thinking together."

Peter Steinberger — AI builder and open source advocate

Steinberger flagged a significant and underreported signal: AI-generated bug reports to the Linux kernel security list have exploded from 2-3 per week two years ago, to ~10/week last year, to 5-10 per day since the start of 2026. A kernel maintainer quoted in the thread noted that most of these reports are technically correct — which is the real problem, since maintainers must now evaluate an order-of-magnitude more volume and have had to bring in additional help. Steinberger's prediction: this AI slop flood is going to kill some open source projects.

Training Data — "How Autonomous Labs Will Transform Scientific Research: Ginkgo Bioworks' Jason Kelly"
Published: March 24, 2026

Takeaway: Jason Kelly believes AI is the first technology revolution in thirty years that will actually change the fundamentals of how science gets done — and that biopharma is about to be disrupted the way every other industry already has been.

Kelly, founder and CEO of Ginkgo Bioworks, joined Training Data to trace his company's path from a bootstrapped idea out of grad school in 2008 to a pioneering force in programmable biology. Ginkgo's core insight: DNA is code — ATCGs instead of zeros and ones — and cells, unlike computers, move atoms rather than information. If you can program cells, you can build almost anything. But doing so has always been brutally hard.

For fifteen years, Ginkgo focused on two parallel problems: making it cheaper and faster to test biological designs in the lab (the "back end" hardware-plus-software challenge), and improving the quality of those designs in the first place (the "front end" modeling challenge). Kelly described their foundry approach — centralized, automated labs designed to accelerate the compile-debug cycle of biological development, where building and testing a DNA design is a physical process requiring chemistry, cell growth, and measurement.

In the AI era, Kelly says he's stepped back from the design-side problem — now well-served by companies like Chai Bio and the Arc Institute's EVO2 genomic model — and is doubling down on autonomous lab execution: can AI agents actually run the physical experimentation loop? Ginkgo's collaboration with OpenAI is pointed directly at this question.

His sharpest line on why this moment is different from every prior tech wave: "All of the previous revolutions in tech — Internet, social media, whatever — have been totally meaningless to biotechnology and biopharma. Like, yeah, it's nice. We communicate slightly better or whatever. It's just some back-office IT crap. Not this. This is actually gonna change the fundamentals of how we do science."

The episode also covers Ginkgo's early YC days — Sam Altman personally persuaded Kelly to apply after Kelly sent him a cold email about the company being "an oasis in the desert" for a bootstrapped biotech that didn't fit the traditional VC mold.
