Aaron Levie (CEO, Box)
Two sharp macro takes from Levie today:

On compute demand: Amazon has deployed more datacenter capex in the last three years than in its entire prior history. Levie points out that today's AI consumption is still dominated by relatively token-efficient chat tools. Coding agents use orders of magnitude more tokens — and that consumption pattern is about to hit all of knowledge work. He expects demand to be "100s of times greater than we're realizing right now."
https://x.com/levie/status/2043192337111924930

On lawyers and AI: Levie argues we'll have more lawyers in the future, not fewer. AI democratizes legal questions (driving demand), creates novel legal complexity, and generates whole new areas of law around AI itself. He cites historical precedent: between 1975 and 2025, despite PCs and the internet making law far more efficient, the number of active attorneys grew from ~400,000 to ~1,375,000. Making a profession more efficient often increases demand for it.
https://x.com/levie/status/2043051227387433144

Garry Tan (President & CEO, Y Combinator)
Tan is pushing back on thick AI harness architectures with a memorable heuristic: "If your memory dies when your harness dies, you built the harness too thick." His principle, drawn from 3 months of open-source agentic tooling used by tens of thousands of engineers: memory is markdown, skills are markdown, the brain is a git repo. The harness should be a thin conductor that reads files — it shouldn't own them. He calls this approach "thin harness, fat skills."

No Priors — "The Agentic Economy: How AI Agents Will Transform the Financial System"
Guest: Jeremy Allaire, Co-Founder & CEO of Circle
https://www.youtube.com/@NoPriorsPodcast

Takeaway: The financial infrastructure that exists today — built around human-paced banking — cannot support an economy where AI agents transact with each other billions of times per second, and stablecoins like USDC may be the only existing technology capable of filling that gap.

Jeremy Allaire, who co-founded Circle in 2013 with a vision of "a protocol for dollars on the internet," connects thirteen years of stablecoin infrastructure-building directly to the emerging agentic economy. His thesis is that AI is collapsing the timeline on a transition he always believed was coming: the financial system needs to be rebuilt for machines, not just people.

Allaire describes the problem clearly: "We don't have an infrastructure that can work globally, interoperably, instantly, that can be programmed through software layers by arbitrary pieces of software. That doesn't exist." His answer is that third-generation blockchains — which only matured in the last couple of years — finally deliver on the original promise. USDC can now settle transactions for a millionth of a penny, at any scale, with no banking hours. That's the only infrastructure capable of supporting an agentic economy where AI agents pay other AI agents for specialized compute, intelligence, or services in real time.

A key quote captures the scale of the shift Allaire anticipates: "AI agents conducting the work, AI agents collaborating with each other, AI agents consuming services from each other and purchasing effectively specialized intelligence or output — the agentic economy is being born as we speak."

Allaire also connects stablecoins to a deeper monetary philosophy: full-reserve money — currency that can't be fractionally lent — is inherently safer than traditional banking, and the recently passed Genius Act codifies this in US law. Circle's USDC holds only short-duration US treasuries or overnight treasury collateral, making it structurally distinct from fractional-reserve instruments.

The episode also touches on Circle's new blockchain Arc, which enables transactions at sub-cent and even sub-millicent levels — essential infrastructure if agents are to micropay each other routinely. Allaire notes that USDC is already by far the most transacted digital currency in the world, with transaction volumes growing faster than the monetary base itself, driven by collapsing transaction costs and rising money velocity.

This is a foundational episode for anyone thinking about the intersection of AI infrastructure and financial infrastructure — and why they may be converging faster than most realize.
