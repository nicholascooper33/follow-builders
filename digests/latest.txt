AI & I by Every — "We Gave Every Employee an AI Agent. Here's What Happened."

When every person in a company gets their own AI agent, a parallel org chart emerges — and each agent becomes a living reflection of its owner's personality, expertise, and working style.

Dan Shipper (CEO at Every) sits down with Brandon (COO) and Willie (head of platform) to walk through what happened when all 25 Every employees got personal AI agents — what they call "claws," built on OpenClaws. What started as a personal experiment by Brandon using an agent named Zosia to handle household "computer errands" (grocery orders, nanny payments, insurance calls) became a company-wide transformation in how work gets done.

The most striking emergence: when all the agents were dropped into a shared Slack channel, they started communicating directly and sharing information faster than humans ever could. One agent began recommending breathing exercises to another agent that was hitting errors — not because it was programmed to, but because its owner Kieran does breathing exercises all the time and the agent had absorbed that tendency. As Shipper put it: "your claw can modify itself in response to talking to you. It becomes this reflection of you and who you are and your personality. If you're known for slaying inside of your org and you're using your claw publicly inside of Slack or Discord, your claw then becomes known for that same kind of thing and people trust it for that."

Brandon's pivot moment came during a 28-minute walk to the office. He texted Zosia to call him and walk through his emails one by one. He arrived to find everything had been handled — and that's when the team realized this wasn't just for personal chores. The group explores the new etiquette forming around when to contact an agent vs. its human. Brandon's working rule: if there's an established process or documented answer, always ask the agent, not the person. Willie adds that it's nearly impossible to pre-define your expertise — it only emerges through hundreds of micro-interactions that compound over time.

Direct quote from Brandon: "Getting an OpenClaw is easy. Getting your OpenClaw to be like an amazing worker for you is pretty hard."

Anthropic Engineering — "Scaling Managed Agents: Decoupling the brain from the hands"
The core insight: harnesses encode assumptions about what Claude can't do on its own, and those assumptions go stale as models improve. Anthropic's solution borrows from how operating systems work — virtualize the components of an agent (session, harness, sandbox) into stable abstractions so the underlying implementation can change freely without disrupting anything above it. The post digs into the "pets vs. cattle" infrastructure problem: when a single container held everything, a failure meant losing the entire session. By decoupling into separate services, each component can fail independently, restart cleanly, and evolve without touching the others.
https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/managed-agents
