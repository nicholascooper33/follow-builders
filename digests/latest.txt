Latent Space — Moonlake: Causal World Models Should Be Multimodal, Interactive, and Efficient — with Chris Manning and Fan-yun Sun
https://www.youtube.com/@LatentSpacePod

Takeaway: Video generation and world models are fundamentally different things — and Moonlake is building the kind of action-conditioned, causally-structured world model that AI agents actually need.

This episode dives deep into one of the most important distinctions in AI right now: the difference between generating video that looks real, and actually modeling how the world works. Chris Manning (Stanford NLP legend) and Fan-yun Sun (Moonlake co-founder) lay out why current video generation models — impressive as Sora and similar systems are — don't constitute real world understanding. As Manning puts it: "You only actually have a world model if you can predict, given some action is taken, what is going to change in the world because of it."

The core thesis of Moonlake is that vision has stalled not because of scale, but because of structure. Current vision-language models let the language side do 90% of the work, while the vision component barely contributes. The fix, according to Manning and Sun, isn't more pixels — it's an abstracted, symbolic layer that captures geometry, physics, affordances, and spatial logic that are invisible at the pixel level.

A key insight is the data problem: collecting observational video online is easy, but it doesn't capture actions and their consequences. To build action-conditioned models, you need interactive data — ideally from simulation, where you control and observe every action. This is the gap Moonlake is filling, building reasoning models that can generate interactive worlds for training embodied AI agents.

Sun's background is telling: during his PhD at NVIDIA research, he was generating synthetic data for multimodal pre-training and showed it matched real-world data quality. The commercial demand for interactive training worlds is exploding, yet most efforts approach it from pure video generation. Moonlake is betting that the right frame is building reasoning models with structural world understanding — "structure and scale," not structure versus scale.

The conversation also surfaces a tension in the field: scale-optimists believe enough observational video will eventually solve spatial intelligence; Moonlake believes you can't get there without action-conditioned data collected at the right level of abstraction. Given the billions being spent on simulation infrastructure across robotics and embodied AI, this research direction has serious commercial relevance.

Direct quote from Chris Manning: "Despite a huge investment in the computer vision space, vision understanding sort of stalled out. You got to object recognition, and then progress just wasn't being made. If you look at any of these vision language models, it's the language that's doing 90% of the work and the vision barely works."
