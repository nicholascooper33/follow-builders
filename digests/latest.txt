Andrej Karpathy — AI researcher, previously Director of AI at Tesla and founding team at OpenAI

Karpathy made a compelling case for personal knowledge wikis as the right model for AI personalization — explicit, user-owned, stored in universal file formats (markdown, images), and portable across any AI provider. Rather than letting an AI company silently accumulate knowledge about you in their servers, a local wiki keeps your data inspectable and interoperable, with agents able to apply the full Unix toolkit over it. He argues four properties matter most: the memory artifact is explicit and navigable, the data stays on your local machine, files are in universal formats (not trapped in a proprietary app), and you can plug any AI into the same data. "Keep the AI companies on their toes!" He describes "agent proficiency" as a core skill of the 21st century.
https://x.com/karpathy/status/2040572272944324650

Karpathy also shared a longer-form take on AI and government accountability. His argument: AI doesn't just give governments new tools to make citizens legible — it gives citizens the tools to make governments legible in return. Laws, spending bills, lobbying disclosures, and FOIA responses are technically public but practically unprocessable. AI dissolves that bottleneck, enabling anyone — not just trained investigative journalists — to parse 4,000-page omnibus bills, track voting patterns against stated positions, or map lobbyist-to-legislator-to-vote graphs. He's cautiously optimistic this increases democratic participation overall, while acknowledging the same tools can cut the other way.
https://x.com/karpathy/status/2040549459193704852

He also shared that his "LLM wiki" tweet went viral and followed up with an "idea file" — a gist-format concept description you hand to your agent, which then builds your own personal wiki customized to your needs. The framing: in the agent era, sharing ideas beats sharing specific apps, since each person's agent can build the tailored version for them.

Aaron Levie — CEO at Box

Levie made a sharp point about why the context layer will always be central to enterprise AI: even the best models can't be trained to know everything every user needs, especially in enterprise settings where each person has different access to sensitive documents. You can't fine-tune a model that "keeps secrets" from some users but not others — it's architecturally untenable. "This is why the context layer is going to always be the core part of the AI stack for applied use cases to turn general models into useful agents. Can't fight the physics on this one."
https://x.com/levie/status/2040650799550722243

In a longer post, Levie traced how agent architecture is finally starting to match how people actually work. A year ago, the state of the art for document-heavy agents was chunked RAG: embed, similarity-search, pull context. It worked, but with awkward edge cases — like when a document says "do not use this" on page 1 but the relevant content is on page 3. With better context windows, tool calling, and reasoning, agents can now search and read documents far more like a human would. His team redesigned Box Agent's entire architecture around this shift: the agent now searches similar to how a user searches, expands queries semantically, and can read many large documents at once. "As model progress continues on the dimensions of context accuracy, tool calling, advanced reasoning, and coding, agents are going to become insanely powerful."
https://x.com/levie/status/2040528801386361128

No Priors — "AI for Atoms: How Periodic Labs is Revolutionizing Materials Engineering with Co-Founder Liam Fedus"

Takeaway: Liam Fedus — co-creator of ChatGPT and former VP of Post-Training at OpenAI — left one of the most coveted roles in AI to build Periodic Labs, betting that the same acceleration AI brought to software will hit the physical world, but only if AI systems are connected to real experiments, not just text.

Fedus traces a natural arc: physics undergrad doing dark matter research → machine learning at Google Brain during the era of the transformer and mixture-of-experts → OpenAI, where he helped turn GPT-4 into ChatGPT → Periodic Labs, where the bet is that materials science, chemistry, and advanced manufacturing are the next frontier.

The Periodic model works in closed loops. AI systems ingest literature and simulation data to build a prior, then design physical experiments, observe results, look for anomalies and patterns, and feed that back into the next round. Language models act as an orchestration layer — directing experiments, processing multi-modal data — while specialized neural nets handle lower-level atomic modeling with symmetry awareness and low latency. A key insight: published materials data is often unreliable, with reported values spanning orders of magnitude. "Once we start moving into specific discovery areas, chemical spaces, we can see a very high level of sample efficiency. The system isn't starting as a randomly initialized neural net — it has a strong prior on the world."

Fedus draws an analogy to early Google Brain: a small number of people and GPUs, high diversity of research, fast iteration — before it became industrialized and driven by scaling laws. His argument is that materials science is at a similar inflection point, where AI plus automation can now run experiments at a throughput that no human team could interpret unaided. The company is starting by treating itself as "customer zero," building tools that internal scientists use before selling to enterprises bottlenecked by materials or process engineering.

The vision: "If our physical world could keep up at some fraction to our digital world, I think life will just feel dramatically different." He compares it to the agricultural revolution — a massive, discontinuous spike in physical-world productivity enabled by the same scaling-law mindset that transformed software.

"Software engineering now looks wildly different than even six months ago. I think we see similar opportunities in the physical world. Of course atoms are hard, and so you will have some limits of physics. But just because atoms are hard doesn't mean there's not an order of magnitude or two to speed up just making sense of huge amounts of data and getting to solutions more quickly."
