Unsupervised Learning — Ep 84: OpenAI's Chief Scientist on Continual Learning Hype, RL Beyond Code, & Future Alignment Directions

Takeaway: OpenAI's Chief Scientist Ako Paioki believes the bottleneck to truly autonomous AI research is not raw intelligence but long-horizon task management — and he sees reinforcement learning expanding well beyond code and math into medicine, law, and science within the next few years.

In a wide-ranging conversation on the Unsupervised Learning podcast, Paioki covered how the explosive rise of AI coding tools — particularly Codex — signals meaningful progress toward the research-intern-level AI capabilities OpenAI has been targeting. He described a clear distinction between a "research intern" (capable of executing specific, well-scoped ideas autonomously) and a "fully automated AI researcher" (able to pursue open-ended goals without human framing). His view is that the former is close; the latter is a few years out.

On the role of math benchmarks: Paioki explained that math has served as an ideal north star for reasoning models because it is simultaneously measurable and arbitrarily hard. Reaching IMO-level problem solving — including IMO Problem 6 — marks a meaningful milestone. But he noted that OpenAI is now shifting focus toward tasks that are "economically valuable" and useful to science, not just competition math.

On RL beyond code: He acknowledged the common concern — that reinforcement learning works beautifully where verification is easy (code, math) but is harder to apply in law, medicine, or finance. His response is that this difficulty overlaps with the challenge of long-horizon tasks generally. Even a well-defined math problem becomes open-ended if you have to work on it for a year. Getting better at extended autonomy and ambiguous reward signals is the shared frontier.

On alignment: Paioki identified generalization — what values does a model fall back on when it encounters novel situations — as the core long-term alignment challenge. He views continual learning not as an ignored problem but as the thing that current training methodologies are, slowly and deliberately, working toward.

"I think we've definitely seen very encouraging signs both on just our ability to scale RL on these more general domains." — Ako Paioki, Chief Scientist, OpenAI
